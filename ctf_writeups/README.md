# CTF Write-ups

A collection of write-ups from Capture the Flag challenges, standalone rooms, and practice machines from platforms like TryHackMe and Hack The Box. These are separate from my structured learning paths — this folder is where I document individual challenges I've worked through on my own.

---

## What is a CTF?

Capture The Flag challenges are essentially security puzzles. You're given a target system, a web app, a file, or a scenario and your job is to find hidden flags — usually strings of text formatted like `THM{some_flag_here}` — by exploiting vulnerabilities, analyzing files, cracking passwords, or working through a chain of steps.

CTFs are one of the best ways to practice real security skills because they force you to think like an attacker. There's no guided walkthrough telling you what to do next — you have to enumerate, research, try things, fail, and try again until something works.

---

## My Approach

Every write-up in this folder follows the same format:

- **Objective** — what the challenge is asking
- **Tools used** — everything I ran during the challenge
- **Methodology** — step by step how I approached it, including dead ends
- **Flags captured** — what I found and how
- **Key takeaways** — what I learned that I'll carry into the next one

I document the dead ends on purpose. The wrong turns are where the real learning happens and I'd rather have an honest write-up than a polished one that makes it look like I knew exactly what I was doing the whole time.

## My Take

CTFs are humbling in the best way. Coming from a background where I was always the one explaining things to other people, sitting in front of a machine that won't give up its secrets and having no idea where to start is a different kind of challenge entirely. But that discomfort is the point — it's where the real growth happens.

Every flag I capture goes in here. Every one I don't capture but learn something from goes in here too. This folder is as much a learning journal as it is a portfolio piece.
