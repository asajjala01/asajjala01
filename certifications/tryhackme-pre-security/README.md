## Why TryHackMe

By the time I finished the Google Cybersecurity Certificate I had a solid foundation of theory — I understood the concepts, the frameworks, the terminology. But there was a gap between knowing something and actually doing it. Reading about log analysis and running a real command in a live environment are two completely different feelings, and I needed the latter.

That's what brought me to TryHackMe. I'd discovered it toward the end of the Google cert and the moment I saw that they had actual virtual machines, real lab environments, and guided rooms where you're actively probing systems and hunting for flags — I knew this was the next step. The Pre-Security path was the natural starting point and it delivered exactly what I was looking for.

---

## What This Path Is About

The Pre-Security path is designed to bridge the gap between knowing security concepts and actually applying them. A lot of what's covered here overlapped with things I'd already studied in the Google certificate — networking, Linux, operating systems, SQL — but the difference was the delivery. Instead of reading about how something works, TryHackMe puts you inside a VM and makes you figure it out hands-on.

For each module they provided a virtual machine loaded with files, systems, and scenarios. The tasks weren't just "answer this question" — they were "go find this file," "probe this system and tell me what you see," "capture this flag." That practical layer on top of familiar concepts made everything stick in a way that reading alone never quite does.

## My Take

Coming off the Google certificate this path felt like stepping out of a classroom and into a lab. Most of the concepts weren't brand new to me — I'd covered networking, Linux, SQL, and security fundamentals already — but that almost made it better because I could focus less on absorbing new information and more on actually doing the work.

The VM-based labs are where TryHackMe really earns it. Being handed a virtual machine and told to go find a specific file, capture a flag, or answer a question by probing the system yourself — that's a completely different experience than any reading or video could give you. There's a moment when you run a command, get the output back, and find exactly what you were looking for that just hits differently. That's the feeling that keeps you coming back.

The attacks and defenses module was a good closer — it was a bit of both familiar and new, and having the practical component behind it made the defensive concepts feel a lot more grounded than they did in theory. Overall this path confirmed everything I already suspected — hands-on is how I learn best, and TryHackMe is where I'm going to be spending a lot of my time.
